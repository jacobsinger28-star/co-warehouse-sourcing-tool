# The Signal Library

The engine is only as good as the signals you feed it. Signals are the
public-record patterns that correlate with an owner who may transact. They are
additive: each one present adds points, the score is the sum, and you can always
read why a property ranked where it did.

Treat what follows as a starter library to tune against your own closed deals,
not as gospel. Turn on the block that fits your asset class, drop it into Stage 2,
and adjust the weights over time.

---

## Universal signals (apply across asset classes)

- **Long tenure (owned 10+ years):** low basis, a digestible tax hit, often
  deferred capital needs. Add more points at 20+ years.
- **Absentee or out-of-state owner:** mailing address is not the property
  address. Distance correlates with willingness to sell.
- **Tax delinquency:** the clearest public marker of pressure. Weight any year;
  weight more for two or more years.
- **Aging mortgage near term:** a loan approaching its balloon, especially into a
  worse rate than the owner locked.
- **Individual or family / trust ownership:** a person who can decide, not a
  committee.
- **Single-asset or small operator:** one or two parcels, not a fund.
- **Recorded liens:** code-enforcement, mechanic's, federal or state tax liens,
  lis pendens in the last 24 months.

---

## Multifamily

Apartment owners telegraph motivation through operations and debt.

- Long tenure with low basis and deferred capital needs.
- Absentee or out-of-state ownership.
- Open code violations or repeat complaints, where the data exists.
- Tax delinquency.
- Aging mortgage nearing its term into a worse rate.
- Small operators holding one or two assets rather than a fund.

In dense markets, the two-to-five-unit and mid-size owners surface months before
a listing ever appears.

## CRE and the maturity wall

Commercial real estate has a clock built into its debt. Conduit (CMBS) loans run
5, 7, or 10-year fixed terms with a balloon at maturity. An owner who closed a
10-year loan in 2014-2016 hits maturity in 2024-2026, into a higher rate. That
maturity wall is the single strongest CRE motivation signal.

- Loan-maturity proximity: score by how close the balloon is and how far rates
  have moved against it.
- Occupancy decline against the submarket trend.
- Special-servicer transfer: the loan is already in trouble.
- Sponsor clusters: one owner carrying several balloons at once.

## Industrial and small-bay

A fragmented, owner-operator market, which is exactly why it sources well.

- Long-tenured individual or family ownership, often the original developer.
- Owners aging out with no clear succession plan.
- Single-asset holders rather than institutional portfolios.
- Functional obsolescence (low clear heights, tight truck courts) in a submarket
  where rents have run.
- Absentee owners managing from far away.

Small-bay rarely trades on open marketing, which is the whole point of sourcing
it this way.

## Manufactured housing (a worked, weighted model)

The cleanest case study for the method. Ownership is overwhelmingly mom-and-pop:
by common industry estimates, of roughly 44,000 US mobile-home parks, more than
40,000 are mom-and-pop owned, and only about 4,000 sit with the top 100
institutional operators. A deep, reachable universe. A representative additive
model:

| Signal | Points | Data tier |
|---|---|---|
| Last sale 10+ years ago | 20 | A |
| Last sale 20+ years ago (add) | +10 | A |
| Individual or personal-trust owner | 15 | A |
| Property tax delinquent (any year) | 25 | A |
| Tax delinquent 2+ years (add) | +10 | A |
| Absentee owner (mailing not situs) | 10 | A |
| Out-of-state owner (add) | +5 | A |
| No recorded mortgage (high equity) | 10 | B |
| Mortgage age 7+ years (balloon window) | 10 | B |
| Recorded code-enforcement lien | 20 | B |
| Federal or state tax lien | 15 | B |
| Lis pendens (last 24 months) | 15 | B |
| Single-park operator (1-2 parcels) | 5 | B |
| Raw code violation (where available) | 5 | C |

Raw code violations sit in the lowest data tier on purpose: coverage runs roughly
20% across rural counties, so treating them as a standard signal would fake a
precision you do not have.

## Land and infill

- Long-hold individual owners of entitlable parcels.
- Owners outside the market with no development activity on the parcel.
- Tax delinquency or holding cost pressure on raw land.
- Adjacency to recent rezonings or assembled parcels nearby.

---

## How to use this

Pick the block for your asset class, paste its signals and points into Stage 2,
and let the additive model rank. Then do the one thing that makes it yours: after
your first wave of outreach, look at which signals the owners who actually engaged
had in common, and reweight. The signals that predicted your real conversations
are the ones worth trusting.
